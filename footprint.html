The footprint,
decoded

The footprint shows aggressors — who initiated. Behind every aggressive trade sits a passive resting order on the other side. A 0 means no aggressor showed up there; it says nothing about whether passive orders were resting.

Aggressor (visible) ↔ Passive (inferred)
01 / 07

The bid × ask convention

Every footprint cell reads bid × ask. The number is traded volume on each side — and each side's volume was put up by the aggressor, leaving the passive counterparty invisible.

BID · left

Volume at the bid

  • Aggressive sellers hit the bid
  • Passive counterparty = resting buyers

ASK · right

Volume at the ask

  • Aggressive buyers lift the offer
  • Passive counterparty = resting sellers

// Read it

0 × 68 = zero aggressive sells, 68 aggressive buys.

! The trap

bid = 0 only proves no aggressive seller traded there. Passive buy orders may or may not have been resting — they leave no trace unless someone hits them.

02 / 07

The four extreme-tick patterns

Judge finished vs rejected at the extreme tick — the top of an up-move, the bottom of a down-move. One rule does the work: the 0 sits on the side that gave up.

Top · finished (passive)

0 × N

Buyers faded

Demand exhausted on its own. Weaker signal.

Top · rejected (aggressive)

N × 0

Sellers slammed it

The opposite side actively took over. Stronger reversal.

Bottom · finished (passive)

N × 0

Sellers faded

Supply exhausted on its own. Weaker signal.

Bottom · rejected (aggressive)

0 × N

Buyers grabbed it

The opposite side actively took over. Stronger reversal.

Quick check

Find the 0 — that's who gave up. If the give-up side is the one you'd expect to dominate at that extreme (sellers at a bottom, buyers at a top) → passive finish. If the opposite side gave up and the aggressor is the reversal side → active rejection.

// Why rejection beats exhaustion

An active takeover is stronger evidence than the dominant side merely running out of steam.

03 / 07

Finished vs unfinished auction

A separate question from the four patterns. Look only at the extreme tick and ask whether one side printed a zero.

Zero on one side

Finished auction

Move completed. No unfinished business — the auction did its job at that extreme.

Both sides printed

Unfinished auction

e.g. 45 × 117. The move was cut off mid-process. Price tends to return to that exact level to finish it.

The magnet behaves directionally

Unfinished at a top pulls price back up to the high. Unfinished at a bottom pulls price back down to the low. It returns to the one unfinished tick — not "above and below."

Platform

MotiveWave sometimes labels this "unfinished business."

04 / 07

Delta — net aggression

Delta = Σask − Σbid for a bar. It measures who was the net aggressor — but it does not tell you who won. Price does. The signal lives in the mismatch between delta and price.

Positive delta

Aggressive buyers were the net aggressors.

Negative delta

Aggressive sellers were the net aggressors.

Divergence vs absorption

Divergence — hollow / weak

  • New high on negative delta → buyers weren't behind it. Hollow high → bearish.
  • New low on positive delta → sellers weren't behind it. Hollow low → bullish.

Absorption — strong (opposite read)

  • Price holds/rises despite heavy negative delta → selling soaked up by passive buyers → bullish.
  • Price holds/falls despite heavy positive delta → buying absorbed by passive sellers → bearish.

// Location breaks the tie

Same numbers, opposite meaning. Never read delta in isolation — location + what price actually did decides.

05 / 07

Diagonal imbalance

Imbalance is diagonal, not same-row. You compare each side against the other side one tick away.

Buy imbalance

Ask at price P vs the bid one tick below P.

Sell imbalance

Bid at price P vs the ask one tick above P.

// Flag threshold

Mark it when the ratio is ≥ 3:1.

Stacked imbalances

Several in a row = a strong directional thrust. Where stacking starts = the base / initiation point (often later support for a buy stack, resistance for a sell stack). Where stacking stops = the exhaustion point. Check every level in a stack, not just the obvious one.

06 / 07

The phantom check

A single-sided print (N × 0 or 0 × N) at an extreme is only real if two things hold.

Confirm before you trust it

Enough volume at that tick for the missing side to mean something — not 1–2 trades by chance.
Price respected it — the next bar fails to trade through the extreme.
Single-sided print
Next bar reaction?
↓
No follow-through → REAL
Trades through → PHANTOM

! When a × 0 lies

It's a phantom when the bar simply closed before any trade printed on that side (bar-boundary timing), or the tick was too thin to matter. Follow-through aggression straight through it = noise, not rejection.

// Rule

No follow-through in the signal direction = real. Trade-through = phantom.

07 / 07

The delta profile

The right-side histogram — Summary Profile with Type = Delta over a custom time span. Each horizontal bar is the net delta at that price level, summed over the window. It's the bar-delta concept rotated onto the price axis: where aggression clustered, not when.

Blue / purple bar

Positive net delta at that level — buyers were net aggressors there.

Red / salmon bar

Negative net delta at that level — sellers were net aggressors there.

Reading the bars

Bar length = magnitude. Flagged numbers (23, -55) = net delta at that level.

How to trade it

Support (bullish)

Heavy sell-delta level that price holds above → absorption.

Resistance (bearish)

Heavy buy-delta level that price falls below → exhaustion.

// The signal

The mismatch between where the aggression happened and where price ended up.
