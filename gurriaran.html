Stance, not method

Why this is a layer on top of everything

My other docs are method tracks: energy/separation (Monroe/Bruce), phasing (Kepple/Tasker), sleep-paralysis (Newport), magical/hermetic (Denning & Phillips). Gurriaran is different in kind — he argues method is the wrong frame entirely. This note is a corrective posture I bring to whichever technique I'm running. The two books are a pair: The Illusion of Method (2021) tears method down; Astral Projection Without Tears rebuilds a minimal practice on the cleared ground.

Why he's the most relevant author to MY actual problem — My bottleneck isn't reaching the vibrational state — I get there. It's tension (bracing when vibrations arrive) and an active controlling mind. Gurriaran's whole thesis is that those two things are the disease, caused by treating projection as something the conscious "I" must do. Three of my sources now point at the bracing/control reflex (Newport: vibrations fade with reps; Kepple: 99% of failures are fear-tension); Gurriaran makes it the entire argument.

The two-selves model

The foundation — from Without Tears

Everything happens inside you, so every participant is a part of you. He splits the Self in two:

I
The conscious selfThe "I" that thinks, desires, imagines, tries. The part reading this.
II
The nervous system — the "other I"Runs heartbeat, temperature, breathing; turns electrical signals into perception; and — crucially — delivers altered states (sleep, and OBEs).

Core claim: OBEs are produced by the nervous system, not the conscious self — exactly like sleep. You cannot make yourself fall asleep by willing it; you can only create conditions and consent. Same with projection.

The direct hit on my blocker — "Why we fail to leave the body" = the conscious self trying to do the nervous system's job. That trying is the tension. Even expectant watching is tension: "if we are expectant we are in a state of mental unrest… tense because we are watchful, waiting for the experience to kick in" — the same way obsessing over falling asleep keeps you awake. That's essentially a description of my bracing-when-the-buzz-arrives problem.

The deconstruction ladder

From The Illusion of Method

Book 1 walks down from the most complex method to the simplest, stripping each as non-essential — to show that none of them is the actual cause.

1
Imaginative techniquesVisualize climbing a rope / leaving the body — the most complex, "top of the pyramid." Reinforce the false belief that I author the OBE.
2
Inner-sound techniquesEar-ringing and the like (see §6).
3
Sleep-offset / dream-state projectionCatch the transition into or out of sleep.
4
Affirmations / belief-shiftingThe simplest method.
5
…but even affirmations aren't the bottomAny method is an arbitrary "ritual" — rope, object, sound, words, sleep-schedule — and they're interchangeable because none is the mechanism. They're costumes the intention wears.

The bottom of the ladder = pure intention + letting go. Mistaking the costume for the mechanism makes you a "slave to the method": anxious you didn't do it right or enough, afraid of failure — and that anxiety is the block.

Honest caveat — don't take "no method" literally — The "you can't project / there is no method" framing is partly rhetorical. He still hands you concrete things to do (ear-ringing, sleep-offset, affirmations), and book 2 is a full step-by-step. In practice it's also a methods book — one that frames methods as disposable scaffolding. Keep practicing; he does. The useful extract is the stance, not a literal instruction to stop doing anything.

The Core Practice

Structured surrender — where both books converge
1
Be fresh and restedNOT tired (see §5). Begin from restfulness.
2
Lie comfortablyA genuinely comfortable position — not the uncomfortable "stay alert" posture.
3
Set the intention once"Instead of falling asleep, I'll have an OBE."
4
Then forget the goalHolding it reintroduces expectant tension.
5
Pretend to fall asleepRelax and consent to the nervous system; doze toward sleep without trying to control anything.
6
The state arrives — exit naturallyThe optimal state comes and you exit without doing a thing.
7
"Didn't work? You probably didn't trust enough."His framing for a miss.

On step 7 — the unfalsifiability problem — "You didn't trust enough" makes the method unfalsifiable: failure is always insufficient faith. Worth noticing even while using the genuinely useful part. Book 2 is more practical about this than book 1.

Practical corrections

New in Without Tears — and testable for me

A · Be rested, not tired

The sharpest practical claim — and it partly contradicts my current WBTB / bedtime windows. Most people fail attempting at night when tired: sleepiness drags them under. But fighting sleepiness creates tension, which blocks the OBE — a double bind. The escape: practice when genuinely rested, so there's no sleepiness to fight. You can let the mind drift for a long time with no risk of dozing and no need to strain for alertness.

My experiment — Test a session when fresh/rested (e.g. after a nap, or a weekend lie-in), not only sleep-deprived WBTB. Removes the tension-from-fighting-sleep.

B · The "valley" — two sweet spots, not one

Falling asleep and waking are the two walls of a valley; full sleep is the floor. The near-sleep liminal state — where OBEs live — exists on both walls.

My experiment — On a natural morning wake, stay still and run the Core Practice on the ascent. Converges with Kepple's "swoosh on waking" and Newport's reality-check-on-wake — three sources point at the wake-up moment.

C · The "map upside down" (Ch. 3) — what NOT to do

He explicitly attacks the popular chain of reasoning: OBEs need body-asleep / mind-awake → so practice at night when tired (wrong) → so lie in an uncomfortable position and do mental activity to stay alert (very wrong). All of that manufactures tension. The discomfort-to-stay-awake trick is exactly backwards.

D · Tabula Rasa (Ch. 5) — two subtle self-sabotages

→
Clinging to past performanceJudging tonight by past results, taking the practice personally, feeling responsible for the outcome. But the outcome isn't yours to author — "all we can do is ask, then rest in openness." Wipe the slate.
→
Wishful thinkingThe craving itself is a form of grasping = tension.

Disposable scaffolding

The concrete techniques he DOES give

Even though the thesis is "no method," these are the rituals he teaches as vehicles for the intention. Use whichever I enjoy — they're interchangeable.

✦
Ear-ringing technique(Ch. 3, book 1) Harness the inner high-pitched ringing already present in silence; rest attention on it as the consent-vehicle. Pairs naturally with my existing internal-roar sensitivity.
✦
Sleep-offset projection(Ch. 5, book 1) Project from the wake-up ascent — the valley's second sweet spot.
✦
Dream-state projection(Ch. 6) Convert a dream / lucid moment.
✦
Affirmations / belief-shifting(Ch. 7) A short repeated phrase as the simplest ritual; "I will have an OBE."

Exit signals he lists (match what I already get) — high-pitched ringing, intense non-physical sensations on the verge of sleep. His instruction: do nothing with them but consent. (Contrast: my habit is to spread the vibrations through the body — gentle and fine, but per Gurriaran even that should stay effortless, never a "doing" aimed at forcing exit.)

Maps onto my blockers

The payoff — diagnosis & the adjustment to test
Tension / bracing when vibrations arrive
DiagnosisThe conscious self trying to do the exit; expectant watching IS tension.
Adjustment to testOn vibration onset: consent, don't steer. Spread is OK only if effortless. Don't "try to roll / push out" — he names this as the classic error.
Active mind / can't quiet
DiagnosisHolding the goal + craving the result = grasping.
Adjustment to testSet intention once, then forget the goal. Wipe past-results scorekeeping (Tabula Rasa).
Vibrations only "time to time"
DiagnosisPossibly fighting sleepiness when tired.
Adjustment to testTry rested, not tired; use the wake-up sweet spot as a second window.

Bottom line for my practice — Gurriaran won't help me reach the buzz — I'm already past that. He targets the precise reason I can't convert it: the trying. Treat both books as one stance. The single most actionable line: at the vibrational state, stop trying to separate — set intention, consent, and let the nervous system do it. The cleanest statement of the passivity fix that's been surfacing across all my sources.
